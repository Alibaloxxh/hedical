**CO-96 means the service is not a covered benefit under your specific insurance plan.** The group code CO (Contractual Obligation) means the provider writes off the amount for in-network care. Unlike CO-50 which says "not medically necessary," CO-96 says "this service is not in your plan's benefit package at all."

If you see CO-96 on your EOB, the first step is to check your plan documents to confirm whether the service is truly excluded or was coded incorrectly.

---

## What CO-96 Means for You

With an in-network provider, a CO-96 denial means:

- **You generally cannot be billed** for the denied amount (CO group code = contractual write-off)
- The service is not covered under your plan's benefits — it is an exclusion, not a medical necessity question
- An appeal based on medical necessity will not work for CO-96 (because the plan excludes it regardless)

<StateVariationCallout title="Out-of-network warning">

If you saw an **out-of-network provider**, CO-96 does not carry the same protection. Out-of-network providers are not bound by a participation agreement, and you may be balance-billed for the full amount. Check your state's out-of-network protections and the No Surprises Act.

</StateVariationCallout>

## Common Reasons for CO-96 Denials

| Reason | Example |
|---|---|
| **Service explicitly excluded from plan** | Cosmetic surgery, experimental treatments, weight loss surgery (some plans) |
| **Plan does not cover the type of service** | Chiropractic care excluded from your specific policy |
| **Service not covered at the location** | Out-of-network facility when your plan only covers in-network |
| **Service performed by non-covered provider type** | Plan covers MDs but not chiropractors or acupuncturists |
| **Service exceeds plan's benefit limit** | Plan covers 12 PT visits per year; claim is for visit 13 |

## CO-96 vs. CO-50: Critical Difference

| Detail | CO-96 | CO-50 |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Service not a covered benefit | Not medically necessary |
| Appeal strategy | Question the exclusion — is it really excluded? | Provide medical necessity evidence |
| Likelihood of overturning | Lower — if the plan truly excludes it | Higher — with strong clinical evidence |
| Fixable by provider | Sometimes — if the code or modifier was wrong | Sometimes — if diagnosis code was wrong |

A CO-96 denial with the wrong CPT code can sometimes be fixed by the provider rebilling with a different, covered code. But if the service is genuinely excluded, no amount of medical necessity documentation will overturn it. See the [CO-50 denial guide](/guides/co-50-denial-code) for the medical necessity appeal process, which is the correct strategy for that code.

## What to Do About a CO-96 Denial

**Step 1: Check your plan documents**

Review your Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC) or full plan document. Look for:
- Specific exclusions listed
- Benefit limits (e.g., "12 PT visits per year")
- Provider network requirements

**Step 2: Ask your provider about the code**

The service may have been billed under a code that your plan categorically excludes, while a related code would be covered. Ask the billing office:
- "What CPT or HCPCS code was submitted?"
- "Is there a different code for the same service that would be covered?"

**Step 3: File an appeal with a different angle**

A CO-96 appeal should argue that the service *is* covered under your plan — not that it's medically necessary. Provide:
- The relevant section of your plan document showing coverage
- Evidence that the service was coded incorrectly
- Documentation that the service falls under a covered benefit category

**Step 4: Request a plan exception (if applicable)**

Some plans allow **medical exception requests** for excluded services if you can demonstrate medical necessity. This is separate from a standard appeal and typically requires a Letter of Medical Necessity from your provider.
